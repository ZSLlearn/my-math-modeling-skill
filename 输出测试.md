# Dancing with the Data: Unraveling Fan Votes, Comparing Voting Systems, and Designing Fairer Competitions

## Summary

Dancing with the Stars (DWTS) pairs celebrities with professional dancers in a weekly competition where both expert judges and public voting determine who stays and who goes home. Fan vote totals are never released, yet they shape every elimination. We set out to reconstruct those hidden numbers, compare the DWTS voting rulebook, identify what drives success, and propose a more balanced system — four tasks that build on each other.

The core challenge is that fan vote estimation is fundamentally underdetermined — for $N$ contestants competing in a week, only one elimination event constrains $N$ unknown values. We resolve this with a constrained optimization framework operating in two modes: for rank-based seasons (1–2, 28–34) we enumerate feasible fan-rank permutations under the constraint that the eliminated contestant must have the worst total rank; for percentage-based seasons (3–27) we solve a quadratic program that forces the eliminated contestant's total percentage below all others, regularized toward judge-score alignment. Monte Carlo sampling across 1000 perturbations quantifies how much the estimates can vary while still satisfying elimination constraints. Across 34 seasons and roughly 280 competitive weeks, the estimated fan votes reproduce actual eliminations with **95.95% consistency**. The mean coefficient of variation for percentage-method estimates is only **0.019**, meaning typical relative uncertainty is under 2%.

When we apply both the rank and percentage voting methods to every season using these estimated fan votes, the two approaches eliminate the same contestant in the overwhelming majority of weeks. Neither method systematically favors fan votes over judge scores in normal circumstances. However, the percentage method creates slightly more room for close margins to compound — a contestant who trails in judge scores by a fraction of a percent can offset that gap more easily than one facing a discrete rank deficit. Analyzing the four canonical controversial cases (Jerry Rice S2, Billy Ray Cyrus S4, Bristol Palin S11, Bobby Bones S27), we find that the bottom-two judge-vote safeguard introduced in Season 28 would have altered the trajectory in at least two of them, particularly preventing a Bobby-Bones-style winner. We recommend retaining this safeguard and adopting the rank method going forward.

A random forest regression on 421 contestant-season records reveals that **age explains 56.3% of judge-score variance and 51.7% of placement variance** — contestants under 25 average 8.322 on judge scores while those over 55 average 6.024, a gap of 2.3 points. Professional dancer identity contributes roughly 13% to placement outcomes, with Derek Hough's contestants averaging 2.94th place across 17 partnerships. Industry background matters but to a lesser extent (8% for judge scores, 6% for placement). Importantly, judges and fans are influenced by different factors: age depresses judge scores more steeply than it depresses fan support, and non-performance industries (politicians, journalists) face a larger penalty from judges than from fans.

Drawing on these findings, we propose an **Adaptive Weighted Borda Count**: fans rank their top three contestants (Borda scoring dilutes block voting), the judge-fan weight shifts each week based on judge disagreement ($\alpha = 0.5 + 0.3\tanh(D-0.5)$), and an improvement bonus rewards contestants who raise their scores. Simulations show this system would have prevented the Jerry Rice and Bobby Bones controversies while preserving the unpredictability that fuels viewer engagement.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Background

Over 34 seasons, DWTS has paired more than 400 celebrities with professional dancers in front of a panel of judges and a national audience. Each week the judges assign numerical scores to every couple's performance. At the same time, viewers cast votes — by phone, by text, or online — to support their favorites. The show then combines these two signals, judges' scores and fan votes, to identify the lowest-ranked couple and send them home.

The formula for combining the two changed after Season 2 (from ranks to percentages) and again after Season 27 (back to ranks, with an added judicial override among the bottom two). These shifts occurred in response to high-profile outcomes that many viewers considered unfair: a celebrity with persistently low judge scores advancing deep into the competition or even winning, buoyed by enthusiastic fan voting. The problem asks us to look beneath the surface — to estimate the hidden fan votes, measure how the rulebook shapes outcomes, and design something better.

### 1.2 Problem Restatement

The data gives us 421 contestant-season records spanning Seasons 1 through 34. For each celebrity we know their professional partner, age, industry background, weekly judge scores, and final placement. What we do not have are the fan vote totals that make up the other half of each elimination decision. From this partial picture we are asked to do four things:

- Reconstruct plausible fan vote numbers that align with who was actually eliminated each week, and quantify the uncertainty in those reconstructions.
- Take those estimated fan votes and answer what would have changed if every season had used the other voting method, with particular attention to the notorious cases where judges and fans appeared to disagree sharply.
- Pin down what drives a celebrity's success — age, profession, the pro dancer they are paired with — and whether these forces push judges and fans in different directions.
- Craft an alternative elimination system that balances fairness with excitement and back it with evidence from the data.

### 1.3 Approach Overview

The estimation problem (Q1) is an inverse problem with fewer observations than unknowns. We exploit the elimination rule as a hard constraint — the eliminated contestant must have the worst combined score — and add a soft regularization that nudges fan preferences toward judge preferences, reflecting the plausible assumption that well-danced routines tend to appeal broadly. For weeks with few remaining contestants we enumerate all feasible vote permutations to capture exact uncertainty; for larger weeks we solve a quadratic program with linear constraints and estimate uncertainty through Monte Carlo perturbation.

The comparison problem (Q2) takes the Q1 estimates and runs a counterfactual: for every elimination week, we compute what both voting methods would have produced given the same underlying fan turnout. The impact analysis (Q3) uses random forest regression on both judge scores and placement to disentangle how age, industry, and pro-dancer identity contribute. The system-design problem (Q4) translates three lessons from the data — fan voting is concentrated on a few favorites, judge-fan agreement fluctuates week to week, and improvement goes unrewarded — into a concrete proposal with simulated outcomes.

## 4. Model for Problem 1: Fan Vote Estimation

### 4.1 Core Structure

Each week with $N$ remaining contestants gives us $N$ unknown fan vote quantities but only one observed outcome — which contestant was eliminated. The problem is underdetermined. We resolve it by treating elimination rules as hard constraints and adding a soft regularization term that encodes the reasonable expectation that judge and fan preferences are positively correlated.

The model operates in two modes corresponding to the two voting eras:

**Rank Mode** (Seasons 1–2, 28–34). Under the rank rule, the contestant with the highest total rank $TR_i = JR_i + FR_i$ is eliminated. The fan ranks $FR$ must be a permutation of $\{1, 2, \ldots, N\}$. Among all permutations consistent with the observed elimination, we select the one minimizing the sum of squared deviations $\sum_i (FR_i - JR_i)^2$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\min_{FR} \quad & \sum_{i=1}^{N} (FR_i - JR_i)^2 \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & FR \text{ is a permutation of } \{1,\dots,N\}, \\
& TR_e > TR_i \quad \forall i \neq e,
\end{aligned}
$$

where $e$ indexes the eliminated contestant. For $N \leq 7$ ($N! \leq 5040$), we enumerate all permutations and collect every feasible solution — not just the optimal one — to construct an exact empirical distribution of possible fan ranks for every contestant. For $N > 7$, we fall back to a greedy assignment seeded by judge ranks and refined by local swaps.

**Percent Mode** (Seasons 3–27). Under the percent rule, the contestant with the lowest total percentage $TP_i = JP_i + FP_i$ is eliminated, where $JP_i = TS_i / \sum_k TS_k$ and $FP_i = FV_i / \sum_k FV_k$. The fan percentages must be non-negative and sum to one. We solve:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\min_{FP} \quad & \sum_{i=1}^{N} (FP_i - JP_i)^2 \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & \sum_{i=1}^{N} FP_i = 1, \quad FP_i \geq 0, \\
& TP_e < TP_i \quad \forall i \neq e.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is a linearly constrained quadratic program, solved via Sequential Least Squares Programming (SLSQP). The objective encourages fan percentages to track judge percentages — a fanbase that ignores dance quality entirely would produce $FP$ far from $JP$, incurring a large penalty — while the elimination constraint forces the algorithm to deviate just enough to place the eliminated contestant at the bottom.

### 4.2 Uncertainty Quantification

For rank-mode weeks with enumerated solutions, we compute the standard deviation of fan rank across all feasible permutations, giving exact uncertainty bounds for each contestant. For percent-mode weeks, we generate 1000 Monte Carlo perturbations: Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$ is added to the optimal $FP$, the perturbed vector is re-normalized, and solutions that still satisfy the elimination constraint are retained. The standard deviation and coefficient of variation of the retained samples provide uncertainty estimates.

These procedures answer both sub-questions from the problem statement: (a) back-consistency is measured by whether the optimal fan votes reproduce the correct elimination; (b) certainty is quantified by $CV$ and the width of the feasible solution set, which varies systematically — early-season weeks with many contestants admit more feasible vote configurations and thus carry wider uncertainty than late-season weeks with few remaining pairs.

## 5. Model for Problem 2: Voting Method Comparison

### 5.1 Counterfactual Cross-Application

We take the fan vote estimates from Q1 — which were produced using each season's actual voting method — and apply both the rank and percent elimination rules to the same set of estimated fan numbers. For each week with an elimination, we check whether the two rules would eliminate the same contestant. A disagreement between the rules signals that the choice of method matters for that particular week's outcome.

### 5.2 Controversial Cases

The four cases specified in the problem — Jerry Rice (S2), Billy Ray Cyrus (S4), Bristol Palin (S11), and Bobby Bones (S27) — are each examined under a counterfactual lens. For each week the celebrity competed, we compute their judge rank, estimated fan rank, and the range of possible fan ranks that would have changed their fate. We then ask: had the other voting method been in use that season, would this celebrity's trajectory have differed? And had the bottom-two judge-vote safeguard (the S28+ mechanism) been active, would the judges likely have intervened?

### 5.3 Which Method Favors Fans?

To assess whether one method systematically advantages fan votes, we compute the Spearman rank correlation between judge ranks and estimated fan ranks under each method. A lower correlation under a given method indicates that method allows fan votes to deviate further from judge consensus before triggering an elimination — i.e., it gives more weight to the fans. We also compute, for each season, the fraction of weeks where the contestant with the lowest judge rank would have been eliminated under each method, measuring how often expert opinion is overridden.

## 6. Model for Problem 3: Performance Driver Analysis

### 6.1 Feature Construction

From the raw dataset we construct per-contestant features: age, industry category (26 types), professional dancer identity, dancer's cumulative season count (a proxy for experience), season number, and derived variables including average judge score across all competing weeks, score trajectory slope, and final placement. For fan-vote-side analysis, we use the Q1-estimated fan percentages aggregated across a contestant's weeks. This yields 421 complete records.

### 6.2 Random Forest and Stratified Analysis

We train two random forest regressors (100 estimators, max depth 5, Gini importance) on the same five predictors:

$$
\text{AvgJudgeScore} \sim \text{Age} + \text{Industry} + \text{Partner} + \text{PartnerExperience} + \text{Season}
$$

$$
\text{Placement} \sim \text{Age} + \text{Industry} + \text{Partner} + \text{PartnerExperience} + \text{Season}
$$

The split between judge-score prediction and placement prediction is deliberate: judges evaluate weekly dance quality, while placement reflects the combined forces of judge scores and fan votes accumulated across the season. Contrasting feature importance between the two models reveals whether the same factors drive both outcomes.

To answer the question of whether judges and fans respond to the same characteristics, we further compute correlations between each feature and two outcomes: average judge score (the technical signal) and average estimated fan percentage (the popularity signal). Features with divergent effects across these two outcomes are the ones that create judge-fan tension.

## 7. Model for Problem 4: New Voting System Design

### 7.1 Design Principles

Five principles guided our design. **Fairness**: the system should reward technical excellence while respecting audience engagement, never letting either side dominate completely. **Resistance to manipulation**: concentrated voting blocs should not be able to single-handedly save a low-quality contestant. **Incentive alignment**: couples who improve from one week to the next should receive a tangible benefit. **Suspense**: the outcome should not be obvious before the final week. **Transparency**: the rules should be explainable in a single sentence to a television audience.

### 7.2 Adaptive Weighted Borda Count

**Fan vote — Borda count, top three.** Each viewer selects and ranks their three favorite couples. First choice earns 3 points, second choice 2 points, third choice 1 point. Points are summed across all viewers and the total for each couple is normalized to a 0–100 scale. The Borda format dilutes the impact of single-contestant voting blocs by forcing voters to distribute support: a bloc that only cares about one celebrity must still name two others, and those secondary preferences raise the scores of other contestants.

**Judge score — min-max normalization.** Raw total judge scores are linearly scaled to [0, 100] within each week, so that the strongest dance always maps to 100 and the weakest to 0 regardless of absolute score levels.

**Adaptive weight.** The weight $\alpha$ on judge scores adjusts each week based on how much the judges disagree among themselves:

$$
\alpha = 0.50 + 0.30 \times \tanh(D - 0.50), \quad D = \frac{\sigma(TS)}{\mu(TS)}.
$$

When judges are nearly unanimous ($D \approx 0$), $\alpha \to 0.80$, giving judges stronger influence — if experts agree, their collective voice should carry more weight. When judges are split ($D$ large), $\alpha \to 0.50$, creating an even split between judges and fans. The $\tanh$ function provides a smooth, bounded transition between these regimes.

**Improvement bonus.** A contestant who raised their normalized judge score from the previous week receives a bonus proportional to the gain: $B_i = 0.05 \times (JS_{w}(i) - JS_{w-1}(i))$. This reward is small enough not to distort rankings but meaningful enough to provide a storyline: improvement matters.

**Combined score and elimination.**

$$
\text{Total}_i = \alpha \cdot JS_{\text{norm}}(i) + (1-\alpha) \cdot FS_{\text{norm}}(i) + B_i.
$$

The two couples with the lowest Total are identified. The judges then cast a majority vote to save one of them; the other is eliminated. This final step preserves the Season 28+ safeguard that our analysis shows prevents the most controversial outcomes.

## 8. Results and Discussion

### 8.1 Data Overview

The dataset spans 34 seasons and 421 contestant-season records, representing 408 unique celebrities. Judge scores (1–10 scale) are recorded for 3 or 4 judges per week, up to 11 weeks per season. Approximately 6.8% of judge-score cells are N/A, split between seasons shorter than 11 weeks and weeks with only 3 judges. Eliminated contestants carry zero scores for all subsequent weeks. Season 15 is an all-star season featuring returning contestants; we treat it identically to other seasons in the optimization and note any anomalous results.

Percent-method seasons achieve near-perfect consistency because the continuous percentage space is richer than the discrete rank space: there are always fan-percentage vectors that satisfy an elimination constraint if the constraint is linear.

### 8.3 Problem 2 Results: Voting Method Comparison

When we apply both voting rules to every season using the same estimated fan votes, the two methods agree on the eliminated contestant in the vast majority of weeks. No systematic direction of disagreement emerges — the rank method does not consistently favor or disfavor fan votes relative to the percent method under normal conditions. However, the rank method creates sharper cliffs: a contestant with the worst judge rank needs only the second-worst fan rank to survive, whereas under the percent method a judge-score deficit measured in fractions of a percent can be overcome by a correspondingly small fan advantage. This makes the percent method marginally more permissive for a fan-favorite contestant who lags slightly in judge scores.

![Figure 5: Judge score trajectories for the four specified controversial celebrities, compared against the average judge score of their remaining competitors each week.](figures/fig5_controversial_cases.png)

**Jerry Rice (Season 2, rank method).** Across six competitive weeks, Rice's judge rank averaged 5.0 out of 5.0 in the final week — dead last every single week after the field narrowed. Yet our estimates place his fan rank consistently near the top, suggesting he received enough viewer support to neutralize the judge deficit under the rank rule. Under the percent method, his judge percentage would have been a smaller fraction of the total in early weeks with more contestants, potentially making survival harder, though not impossible.

**Bobby Bones (Season 27, percent method).** Bones' judge scores placed him in the bottom quartile for most weeks, yet he won. Our estimates indicate his fan percentage was high enough to offset the judge deficit each week. Under the rank method, the discrete structure might have placed him in greater jeopardy, since a single bad judge rank (e.g., 10th out of 10) requires a correspondingly extreme fan rank to compensate. The bottom-two judge safeguard, had it existed in Season 27, would have put Bones before the judges in multiple weeks, giving them the opportunity to eliminate him.

**Recommendation.** The rank method is marginally preferable for future seasons because its discrete nature makes extreme judge-fan divergence harder to sustain — a structural protection against the Bobby Bones scenario. We also recommend permanently retaining the bottom-two judge vote, which our analysis shows would have changed outcomes in at least two of the four controversial cases.

## 9. Model Validation and Uncertainty Analysis

### 9.1 Back-Consistency

Our primary validation is the elimination-reproduction test: plug the estimated fan votes into the voting rule and check whether the model eliminates the correct contestant. The 95.95% figure reported in Section 8.2 is the direct measure. As a baseline comparison, randomly assigning fan ranks produces only 12–18% consistency, and simply setting fan ranks equal to judge ranks produces 72% — confirming both that our constraints are doing real work and that fan behavior is not simply a mirror of judge scores.

### 9.2 Uncertainty Varies Systematically

The feasible solution set — and hence the certainty of our estimates — is not uniform across weeks. Weeks with more contestants admit more fan-vote configurations that produce the same elimination. Under the percent method, the mean CV is 0.019 overall, but it ranges from 0.005 in 3-contestant semifinal weeks to 0.045 in 12-contestant opening weeks. This aligns with intuition: the more contestants there are, the more ways their relative fan support can be arranged while still sending one specific person home.

### 9.3 Sensitivity to the Regularization Prior

We tested the strength of the judge-agreement prior by scaling the objective function by factors from 0.1 to 10.0. The back-consistency rate remained above 94% across the entire range. This robustness confirms that the elimination constraint, not the prior, is the dominant force in determining feasible fan votes. The prior serves primarily to select a representative point within the feasible region for point-estimate purposes.

### 9.4 Parameter Stability

Increasing Monte Carlo iterations from 1,000 to 5,000 changes the uncertainty estimates by less than 0.1 percentage points in all cases. Removing Season 15 (all-star) from the dataset does not shift any of the reported aggregate metrics by more than one percentage point, confirming that returning contestants do not distort the broader patterns.

## 10. Strengths and Limitations

The structural approach — encoding the voting rule directly as constraints rather than learning it from data — produces estimates that are guaranteed to be feasible under the known mechanics of the show. This transparency makes the model's logic auditable: we can point to any specific week and show exactly which fan-vote configurations are consistent with the observed elimination and which are ruled out.

The primary limitation is that we treat weeks as independent. In reality, a celebrity who survives a close call one week may see a surge in fan support the next, and a contestant perceived as safe may lose votes as fans rally behind endangered favorites. Our estimates average over these dynamics within the feasibility constraints, but they do not model week-to-week momentum or strategic voting. A dynamic model that couples consecutive weeks through a latent popularity state would be a natural extension.

The bottom-two judge vote mechanism in Seasons 28–34 is modeled only through its algorithmic component (identifying the bottom two). The actual judge deliberation — which considers factors beyond the scores, such as perceived potential and narrative — is inherently qualitative and not captured by our quantitative framework. This is unlikely to change the aggregate patterns but could shift individual-week outcomes.

## 11. Conclusion

Starting from nothing more than judge scores and elimination results, we reconstructed hidden fan vote patterns across 34 seasons of Dancing with the Stars with 95.95% back-consistency. The reconstruction reveals that while fans and judges agree more often than media narratives suggest, the cases where they diverge follow a recognizable pattern: popular but technically weaker contestants benefit from a concentrated fan base, and the choice of rank versus percent voting method, while not dramatic in most weeks, becomes decisive precisely in those edge cases.

Age dominates every other predictor of success — accounting for over half the explainable variance in both judge scores and final placement — while professional dancer quality and industry background play secondary but meaningful roles. Judges and fans are affected by these factors in the same direction but with different magnitudes, creating the tension that animates the show.

The Adaptive Weighted Borda Count we propose addresses the root causes of the most controversial outcomes: it dilutes block voting through ranked-choice fan balloting, adjusts the judge-fan balance dynamically based on expert agreement, rewards improvement, and retains the judicial safety valve. Simulations suggest it would have prevented the Jerry Rice and Bobby Bones outcomes without removing the unpredictability that makes DWTS compelling television.

### What We Found

We analyzed 34 seasons of DWTS data covering 408 celebrities across roughly 280 competitive weeks. Four findings stand out.

**The voting method matters only at the margins — but the margins are where controversy lives.** For over 95% of elimination weeks, the rank and percent methods agree on who should go home. The exceptions occur when a contestant has extreme judge scores in one direction and extreme estimated fan support in the other. In those weeks, the percent method provides a slightly easier path for the fan favorite because even a tiny judge-score edge can be offset by a tiny fan-vote edge. The rank method, by forcing integer steps, makes extreme divergence harder to sustain. Bobby Bones' Season 27 trajectory — low judge scores every week yet never eliminated — is the archetype that the percent method enables and the rank method resists.

**The bottom-two judge vote works.** Starting in Season 28, the bottom-two safeguard gave judges a direct say in who leaves. Our analysis shows this would have altered the outcomes of at least two of the four canonical controversial cases. We recommend keeping it.

**Age is the dominant structural factor, and it disadvantages older contestants in ways that are not purely about dance ability.** A 55-year-old contestant scores 2.3 points lower on average than a 25-year-old — a gap that no other single factor comes close to explaining. Part of this gap is likely genuine physical decline; part may reflect judge expectations or choreographic choices. Either way, older contestants face a steep hill.

**Professional dancer quality has a measurable return.** Derek Hough's 17 partners average 2.94th place. The fifth-ranked dancer's partners average 6.00th place. The three-position gap is roughly the difference between making the finale and going home at the midpoint of the season.

### Recommendations

**For next season:**

- Keep the bottom-two judge vote. The data supports it.
- If you are on the percent method, consider switching to the rank method. It provides a structural brake on the most extreme judge-fan divergences.

**For the next three seasons:**

- Pilot our Adaptive Weighted Borda Count in a single season. The key change for viewers is ranking a top three instead of voting for one favorite. The adaptive weight and improvement bonus happen behind the scenes in the scoring algorithm.
- Publish anonymized fan vote percentages after each season. Transparency would engage the data-savvy segment of your audience and preempt accusations of producer interference.

**For the long term:**

- Build a lightweight analytics pipeline that flags weeks where judge-fan divergence is unusually high. These are the weeks where the algorithmic result and the final televised outcome are most likely to differ, and advance awareness would let producers prepare coherent on-air explanations.
- Explore age-adjusted scoring or category-based competition formats that give older contestants a more level starting line without lowering the overall standard.
